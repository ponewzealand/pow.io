## Abstract

Internal waves have been observed to mitigate thermal stress on coral reef ecosystems through the shoaling of internal waves that can bring cooler subsurface water onto shallow reefs. Here, we analyse bottom-mounted temperature logger data from two sites north and south of the Poor Knights Islands on the northeast shelf of New Zealand to investigate the role of internal wave activity in modulating reef temperatures. High-frequency temperature variability associated with internal waves is strongly enhanced during summer and correlates closely with stratification, while exhibiting only weak correlation with local wind forcing, suggesting that while wind contributes to setting the background stratification, the instantaneous variability is primarily modulated by stratification. Internal wave signals are more pronounced at greater depths, consistent with closer proximity to the seasonal thermocline. This behaviour is further captured by a simple numerical model, demonstrating how the relative position of a sensor with respect to the thermocline critically controls the amplitude and shape of observed temperature fluctuations, highlighting the sensitivity of in situ observations to vertical sampling. Despite pronounced temperature oscillations, internal waves at our sites do not produce significant net cooling or warming, but exhibit slight negative skewness, indicating a bias towards episodic cooling without a sustained mean temperature change. In doing so, they introduce high-frequency thermal variability superimposed on a slowly varying background state. We propose that this variability, rather than net temperature change, may enhance the resilience of reef organisms to thermal stress.
